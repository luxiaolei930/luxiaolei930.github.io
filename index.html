About the Lab

T&I NUS is a research lab at the Department of Chinese Studies, National University of Singapore (NUS), directed by Prof. Chao Han. The lab is affiliated with the Translation & Interpreting Research Group at the Wan Boo Sow Research Centre, the Centre for Computational Social Science and Humanities at FASS, and the NUS Artificial Intelligence Institute.

We are an interdisciplinary team working at the intersection of Translation Studies, Language Assessment, and Natural Language Processing. By integrating psychometric modeling, cognitive process tracing, and computational techniques, we investigate both manual and automated evaluation of human- and machine-generated translation and interpreting (T&I).

Our programmatic research agenda centres on Quality Assessment in Multilingual, Multimodal, and Multiagent Translation and Interpreting (QAM³T&I) — advancing evaluation methodologies across languages, communication modes, and agent types for T&I training, professional certification, language learning, and the language services industry.

Projects

Cognitive Processes of Human Evaluators

Modeling mental operations and decision-making strategies in assessing human- and machine-generated T&I outputs, using process tracing methodologies such as eye-tracking, keystroke logging, and think-aloud protocols.

Reception and Processing of T&I Products

Investigating how readers and listeners comprehend human- and AI-generated T&I through computational psycholinguistic approaches and multimodal behavioral data.

LLM-based Automated T&I Evaluation

Applying, configuring, and fine-tuning large language models for automated quality evaluation, with a focus on reliability, alignment with human judgment, and validity.

Human–AI Collaboration in Language Evaluation

Examining synergistic integration of human expertise and machine intelligence in formative feedback, summative evaluation, and instructional contexts.

Meta-research in T&I and Language Assessment

Conducting systematic reviews, bibliometric analyses, and meta-analyses to trace methodological evolution and inform evidence-based scholarship.
